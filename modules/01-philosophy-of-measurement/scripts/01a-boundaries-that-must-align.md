## Opening

Most carbon metrics tell you how bad things are. The SCI was designed to tell you what to do about it.

That might sound like a small difference, but it's actually everything. It's the difference between a metric that sits in an annual report and a metric that changes how engineers build software every day.

So let's talk about why that difference exists and how we designed for it.

---

## Part A: Boundaries That Must Align

When you're designing a metric, or when you're choosing which metric to use, there are two boundaries you need to think about.

The first is the **inclusion boundary**. What does the metric actually count? What's inside the box and what's outside?

The second is the **agency boundary**. What can the team who's using this metric actually control? What levers can they pull?

Here's the key insight: where those two boundaries overlap is where the right actions emerge. The actions you want to incentivise live in that intersection.

If you design a metric where the inclusion boundary sits neatly within the agency boundary, you get a metric that drives action. If they don't overlap properly, you get a metric that just makes people feel bad about things they can't change.

**[VISUAL: Two overlapping circles — inclusion boundary and agency boundary. The overlap area is highlighted as "Actions that drive change"]**

Let me give you a concrete example of what happens when these boundaries don't align.

Imagine you give your engineering team a metric: total carbon emissions for your software product.

What's in the inclusion boundary? Everything. The servers, the energy, the hardware, and critically, the usage. The more people use your product, the more emissions it generates.

But what's in the engineering team's agency boundary? They can make the code more efficient. They can optimise infrastructure. They can reduce energy per transaction.

What they can't control is how many people use the product. That's the sales team's job. The sales team has the most agency over product adoption and growth.

So what happens? The engineering team does everything right. They make the software dramatically more efficient. Carbon per user drops by 30%. But the sales team also does their job brilliantly, and the user base doubles.

The total emissions go up. The engineering team's metric gets worse, even though they did exactly what we wanted them to do.

**[VISUAL: Engineering team looking frustrated as their "total emissions" metric rises despite efficiency improvements]**

That's a broken metric. The inclusion boundary doesn't sit within the agency boundary.

Now flip it around. What if we gave the engineering team carbon per user as their metric? And what if the sales team was accountable for total emissions?

Suddenly the engineering team has a metric they can actually improve. Carbon per user is something they have agency over. They can set targets. They can track progress. The C-suite can set year-on-year reduction goals that make sense.

And the sales team? Well, they do have the most agency over total emissions, even if reducing it seems unlikely given their goals. But perhaps they should be the ones investing their profits into carbon neutrality, renewable energy purchases, and offset programmes. They're the ones driving the growth.

The agency of the team should be associated with the inclusion boundary of the metric. When that alignment exists, the metric drives action. When it doesn't, it just creates frustration.
